There's a term floating around developer communities lately: "AI depression." It's not a medical diagnosis—you won't find it in any textbook. It's more of an in-group shorthand for the chronic fatigue, anxiety, and eroding sense of competence that comes with working alongside AI all day. As someone who spends at least eight hours a day surrounded by AI tools, I often don't want to hear the word "AI" outside of work hours.

There was an expectation that AI would help us build faster and try more things. That expectation hasn't entirely disappeared. But over time, it's become harder to ignore the anxiety growing alongside it. The vibe coding approach I use, the models, the tool combinations—none of it feels like the best option. There's a persistent sense of falling behind. Even on days when I've worked hard, something doesn't feel settled.

## This isn't a willpower problem

After months of observing myself and the people around me, I've come to believe this feeling is closer to a **structural problem** than a personal failing.

Here's what the structure looks like:

| Layer | How it works | What it feels like |
|---|---|---|
| **Platform** | Rapid updates, subscription/usage pricing, ecosystem lock-in | "If I don't use it, I'll fall behind" |
| **Organization** | Faster deadlines, output-centric evaluation, individual review responsibility | "Work got faster but I'm more tired" |
| **Peers** | Community comparison, demo/benchmark showing off, latest-stack pressure | "I'm the only slow one" |
| **Individual** | Blurred boundaries, learning overload, shaken self-efficacy | "I'm anxious even when resting" |

Platforms like OpenAI and Claude keep releasing new models and features. Peer groups normalize comparison. Many of us—myself included—subscribe to multiple premium plans out of genuine interest and a fear of being left behind. Within organizations, individuals shoulder learning, production, review, and responsibility simultaneously. The process of directing AI and reviewing its output often isn't recognized as "real work," making it hard to even notice when you're overworked.

Through all of this, work speed may increase, but mental energy depletes faster.

## A bottomless bucket

What individuals end up in is a **deficit-fulfillment loop**:

```
Fear of falling behind → Over-invest in AI (learning, experiments, multi-subscriptions) → Brief relief
  ↑                                                                                        ↓
Greater anxiety ← Increased comparison exposure ← Rising baseline
```

Many developers study after work, run side projects, and spend personal money on tools—not out of excess motivation, but as a rational survival strategy in an uncertain market. But the more you invest out of anxiety, the higher the baseline rises, and the greater the anxiety that follows the brief moment of relief.

This is why it feels like pouring water into a bottomless bucket.

The distinction matters: when the reward is tied to external validation and comparison, the investment acts as a painkiller. When it shifts to internal standards and boundaries, it becomes an asset. Can you move from chasing tools and models toward "less volatile capabilities"—principles, evaluation, reproducibility, safety? Can you redefine the basis of comparison from "latest tech" to "problem solved"? These were the questions that mattered.

## Who pays the cost?

What I really want to challenge is what comes next.

When this private investment stops being a "choice" and becomes effectively mandatory, the cost of learning and the risk of failure shift entirely to the individual. Good performance requires subscriptions. FOMO rationalizes multiple subscriptions. The result: part of the cost of doing your job is transferred to personal spending.

The same applies to time. The loop of directing AI, reviewing output, and re-directing doesn't reduce work—it tends to **increase work density and speed**. The responsibility for AI-generated output ultimately stays with the user. That's why you end up in a state of being "tired without feeling like you worked."

When individuals burn through money and mental energy to work this way, organizational productivity goes up. More measurement is needed, but the trend is clearly felt.

## How I'm coping

There's only so much an individual can do about structural problems. Still, I've been trying a few things, and some have worked.

### Separating emotion from fact in my records

The first thing I did was **consciously separate emotion from fact when recording my day**.

I dump every thought that comes up during the day into a file called Daily Buffer. "Feels close to burnout from comparison fatigue" goes in, and so does "Fixed mobile capture to single input source." The problem is that when these get mixed together, I can't tell what's fact and what's emotion when I look back later. When emotion masquerades as fact, it distorts judgment.

So at the end of each day, I sort the Buffer. Facts—what I did, what was blocked, how I solved it—go to the work log. Emotions—what felt anxious, what felt good—go to the retrospective section. Stray ideas go to the inbox. I started doing this manually, then recently built an AI-powered classifier.

The effect was clear. **Once emotion was separated from the record, the emotion got smaller.** When anxiety gets reduced to a note that says "something I felt today," the vague fear of "I'm falling behind" transforms into a specific observation: "I felt comparison fatigue about this particular thing today." What you can observe, you can manage.

### Redefining the basis of comparison

The root of feeling like "I'm the only slow one" was a miscalibrated comparison target. The people visible on Twitter are the top 1% in their area. Comparing myself to them is like watching the Olympics and wondering "why can't I run like that?"

I changed the benchmark. Not "industry latest" but "me last month." Did my problem-solving patterns improve? Am I making better decisions in the same amount of time? By this standard, most months show progress.

## A call for structural change

Individual coping has its limits. What fundamentally needs to change is the structure.

**What organizations can do**: institutionalize AI learning and experimentation within work hours—framing it as "investment during work" rather than "studying after work." Include quality, reproducibility, and stability in evaluation metrics alongside speed. Fund AI tool costs through team budgets instead of expecting personal spending. Distribute the review responsibility for AI-generated output across processes, not individuals.

**What communities can do**: shift from a culture of demo and benchmark showing off to one of sharing failures. Move conversations from "this tool is the best" to "here's how I solved this problem." Create safe spaces where burnout experiences can be shared.

**What individuals can protect**: set boundaries between work time and learning time. Create a "things I've decided not to do" list—you don't need to use every tool. And record. When you separate emotion from fact in your records, the real shape of anxiety becomes visible.

## Closing thoughts

I believe AI depression is not individual weakness but a social signal sent by the current technology competition system. At a time when things are more likely to get worse than better, I'd like to have a conversation about what mindsets and social mechanisms we need so that individuals don't collapse.

At the very least, I've come to believe that the answer is not "try harder" but "a workflow that depletes less and doesn't break." Instead of pouring more water into the bottomless bucket, I'm looking for ways to patch the bottom.

## FAQ

**Q. Is "AI depression" an actual medical term?**
No. It's not a formal diagnosis. It's closer to community shorthand for the chronic fatigue, anxiety, and diminished self-efficacy that come with heavy AI tool usage. Academically, it maps most closely to the concept of technostress.

**Q. Does vibe coding make AI burnout worse?**
Vibe coding itself isn't the problem. But the endless comparison of "is my setup optimal?" and the concentration of review responsibility on individuals can accelerate burnout.

**Q. Should developers stop using AI for their mental health?**
Not using AI isn't realistic. The key isn't usage volume but boundary-setting. Separating work time from learning time and creating a "things I won't do" list has been more effective.
